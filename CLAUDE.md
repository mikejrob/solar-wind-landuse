# Project: Political Economy of Hawaii's Agricultural Land Restrictions on Solar

## Research question

Who benefits from, and who has shaped, the restrictions on utility-scale solar
development on Hawaii's agricultural lands (HRS § 205-4.5 and § 205-2), and what
does the documentary record show about the role of the incumbent utility
(Hawaiian Electric / HEI) and large institutional landholders (trusts, estates,
legacy ag corporations) in creating and defending those restrictions?

Working hypotheses (to be TESTED, not assumed):
1. Scarcity-rent hypothesis: the per-parcel 10%/20-acre cap restricts the supply
   of solar-eligible land, concentrating rents on large landholders who own
   grid-adjacent parcels and can navigate the Special Use Permit (SUP) process.
2. Rate-base hypothesis: siting friction slows independent power producer (IPP)
   development, favoring utility-owned generation/storage (Averch-Johnson).
   Must engage with performance-based regulation (PUC Docket 2018-0088) —
   did PBR change the incentive?
3. Astroturf hypothesis: apparent grassroots opposition to solar is organized,
   funded, or legally resourced by interested parties (utility, landholders,
   ag lobby). WEAKEST-EVIDENCED hypothesis so far. One counterexample already
   found (HB 2665, 2018 — organic resident testimony).

## Evidentiary discipline (non-negotiable)

- Every factual claim in the paper links to a primary source: session laws,
  committee reports, testimony PDFs, docket filings, lobbyist registrations,
  campaign-finance records, proxy statements.
- Document REVEALED CONDUCT (who testified, who intervened, who represents whom,
  who donated). Never assert unobservable INTENT unless a document states it.
- Names real organizations → defamation-adjacent risk. Report conduct; let
  readers infer motive. A null result (opposition is organic; landholders are
  passive beneficiaries) is a publishable finding and must be reported honestly.
- Three tiers for any inherited claim (esp. anything from the earlier
  Gemini-generated material): (1) verified w/ primary source, (2) plausible
  hypothesis awaiting evidence, (3) unverified assertion — treat as possibly
  confabulated until confirmed. Bill numbers, dollar figures, and
  board-interlock claims from LLM output are Tier 3 by default.

## Verified findings to date (July 2026)

Statutory structure:
- HRS § 205-4.5 enacted L 1976, c 199. Enumerated-use regime: uses not listed
  are excluded from class A/B ag soils by default → solar was effectively
  banned on good ag land from 1976 until 2011 except via § 205-6 special
  permit ("unusual and reasonable uses"). Case law (cell-tower case) shows
  strict reading against utilities.
- Wind asymmetry: RESOLVED (see notes/wind-statutory-entry.md). Wind entered
  via Act 24, SLH 1980 (HB 2418-80, signed 4/17/1980, Ariyoshi): permitted use
  in ag districts, NO cap, NO soil-class ban, only "compatible with
  agriculture uses and cause minimal adverse impact" — original 1980 language
  never substantively amended (renumbered (12)→(14) 2006, (14)→(15) 2012).
  Entered BEFORE any HI wind farm existed; PURPA (1978) contemporaneous; HEI
  corporate history ties early-80s holding-co formation to wind ambitions
  (Kahuku) — utility role in 1980 act PLAUSIBLE BUT UNVERIFIED; 1980 House/
  Senate Journals (paper only: State Archives, LRB, UH law library) needed for
  sponsors/testimony. Curtis v. Bd. of Appeals (1999) treats wind's express
  listing as the model of permitted-as-of-right — the treatment solar never
  got until 2011, capped.
- Current solar provisions: § 205-4.5(a)(20) — permitted use if ≤10% of parcel
  or ≤20 acres, whichever is LESser; banned on class A soils. (a)(21) — SUP
  path on B/C soils requires land also made available for compatible ag at
  ≥50% below fair-market rent, decommissioning security, restoration.
  Parallel language in § 205-2(d)(6).

Legislative timeline (anchor: amendment list in HRS annotations):
- 2008, Act 231: solar first entered §205-2(d)(6) as permitted use on D/E
  (marginal) lands only — per DOA 2011 testimony, deliberately kept off
  higher-productivity soils. So the 1976–2011 "ban" story applies to A/B/C.
  [Act 231 bill number/history to verify.]
- 2011, Act 217 (SB 631): RESOLVED (see notes/sb631-2011.md). 10% cap
  inserted by SENATE (SD1, SSCR489, ENE/AGL/WLH — Gabbard/Nishihara/Dela
  Cruz): B/C/D/E only, 10% of parcel on B/C, IAL ban. 20-acre lesser-of
  ceiling added by HOUSE EEP/WLO (HD1, HSCR1152 — Coffman/Chang), expressly
  sized to ~5 MW = utility competitive-bid waiver threshold for IPPs.
  Cap constituency on the record: Office of Planning, DOA, Honolulu DPP,
  Farm Bureau (flipped opposition→support once 10% cap in; DOA: "would not
  oppose... 'C' rated... with an acreage limitation"). HECO, LURF, KS: NO
  testimony at any hearing. Castle & Cooke SUPPORTED the uncapped bill.
  Sierra Club opposed liberalization throughout (food security). Introducers:
  Solomon, Ige, Kahele, Kidani, Kim, Slom. Signed 7/11/2011.
- 2014 (see notes/acts-2014-2022.md): Act 52 = SB 2775 (signed 2014-04-28,
  NOT 4-30; amended only §205-4.5(a)): HARC/rancher-driven class-A road
  exception; agencies (LUC/HDOA/DBEDT) added SUP/easement conditions +
  sunset 2019-06-30; grandfathering for operable life. Act 55 = SB 2658:
  introduced as PURE LOOSENING (30% cap, no 20-acre limit); Senate WTL
  (Solomon) tightened; SUP-above-cap overlay inserted in SD2 on LUC
  (Orodenker) recommendation; the (a)(21) 50%-below-FMR ag co-use lease
  condition was REQUESTED BY FIRST WIND SOLAR GROUP (developer-tenant on KS
  land), crediting drafting help from LUC and Sierra Club (which never filed
  testimony); decommissioning/restoration added by committee, modeled on
  wind law. Zero opposition testimony on either 2014 bill. Honolulu DPP
  called the rent condition unadministrable — ignored.
- 2015 Act 228 (HB1273, hydro), 2016 Act 173 (HB2077, hydro), 2018 Act 49
  (HB2651, wireless), 2021 Act 77 (HB247, ag CPR), 2022 Act 131 (HB1992,
  composting), 2024 Act 182 (HB1923, camps), 2025 Act 255 (HB969,
  landfills): NONE changed solar siting rules — they add other uses and
  reprint the solar paragraphs verbatim (verified against enacted texts).
  Current regime = 2011 cap + Act 55 (2014) SUP overlay. LURF, HECO/HEI,
  and KS made ZERO testimony appearances in any hearing examined 2014–2025.
- 2018, HB 2665: SUP requirement for solar in nonconforming ag-zoned
  residential subdivisions (D/E lands, Big Island). Supporting testimony =
  individual Ocean View residents (organic); Dept of Agriculture testified
  with strong concerns. Counterexample to astroturf hypothesis.
- 2021, SB 942: RESOLVED — never heard; died in AEN/EET without a hearing
  (lead sponsor Gabbard chaired AEN). No testimony exists, so no
  interest-group lineup is observable for the PPA/BOA-attestation
  chokepoint design.
- State's own Act 278 study (2020, DBEDT/Office of Planning) documents that
  2014 amendments allowed solar by SUP on B/C lands and that solar "confers
  higher land values with much greater revenue" — official acknowledgment of
  the rent wedge.
- 2026, Act 24 (signed 2026-05-21): $40M annual cap on RETITC from 2027,
  sunset 2030. Documented driver = state budget shortfall (~$3B federal
  revenue loss), NOT utility lobbying — do not overclaim. EO 26-02
  (2026-06-11) exempts 2026 systems.

Context (verified):
- Kamehameha Schools: state's largest private landholder; ~40% of commercial
  renewable energy capacity sits on KS land as of 2022 (Kawailoa solar+wind,
  Waiawa, etc.). Consistent with scarcity-rent hypothesis (KS benefits from
  restrictions binding on OTHERS' land), inconsistent with naive
  "landowners oppose solar" framing. Keep this straight.
- HECO Oahu Stage procurement: 20–29 IPP contracts, $2.5–4B investment,
  ~3,000 acres (Civil Beat 2020 reporting).
- Genuine organic opposition exists (Kahuku / Na Pua Makani wind: ~200
  arrests 2019). Contrast pattern: mass mobilization vs wind; professionalized
  legal intervention vs solar. Countable, reportable contrast.

   1980 (HB 2418-80), uncapped from birth. Remaining: 1980 sponsors/testimony
   exist only in paper House/Senate Journals (State Archives, LRB rm 005, UH
   law library) — archive visit needed; HEI/Kahuku-wind role PLAUSIBLE BUT
   UNVERIFIED.
2a. NEW — county layer: Honolulu Ord 25-2 (Bill 64 2023, signed 1/3/2025)
   sets wind setback max(10x tip height, 1.25 mi) from residential/country/
   resort lots, bans DPP waivers, caps repowering; 18/20 Kahuku turbines
   nonconforming; all Oahu wind (~155 MW) expected retired 2031–2040
   (notes/wind-setbacks.md). Escalation chain Res 19-305 (5 mi) → Bills
   28/29/30 (2021) → Bill 10/55 (2022) → Bill 64 documented. State bills:
   HB 2188 (2020) 1-mile — HECO was SOLE organizational opponent (utility
   publicly pro-wind-siting); SB 957 (2021) never heard.
   FRAMING (Mike, 2026-07-11): don't cast this as one ag-land asymmetry story.
   The binding constraint differs by technology and legal layer: solar's is
   the ag-land statute (ag land is especially well suited to solar — flat,
   cleared, grid-adjacent; caps bind exactly there); wind's is
   proximity-to-people rules (setbacks/noise/viewshed — wind wants ridgelines
   and tradewind corridors, not prime soils; the ag statute was never wind's
   chokepoint). Kahuku turbines WERE genuinely close to homes — organic
   grievance — but the enacted response (10x/1.25mi, no waivers, repowering
   cap) is calibrated far beyond the grievance and makes wind infeasible
   island-wide. Quantify: setback geometry vs Oahu parcels (follow-on GIS
   task), and solar-eligible acreage under 10%/20ac vs a hypothetical
   20%-no-hard-cap rule (Mike: "would make a world of difference").
2b. RESOLVED — Act 182 (2024) = HB1923 (camps) and Act 255 (2025) = HB969
   (landfills); neither touches solar/wind siting (solar paragraphs
   reprinted verbatim).
3. Pre-2011 failed solar-liberalization bills (~2005–2010): enumerate, and

4. LURF (Land Use Research Foundation of Hawaii — large-landowner trade

5. HEI board / trust interlocks: check HEI proxy statements vs KS trustee

6. RESOLVED 2026-07-11 (notes/sup-census.md, data/sup_census.csv): the

## Work plan

Phase 1 — Bill inventory (2005–2026):
Scrape capitol.hawaii.gov for every measure amending or attempting to amend
§§ 205-2 / 205-4.5 re solar/renewable siting, passed AND failed. For each:
bill no., year, sponsors, introduced-vs-final delta on siting language,
committee referrals, hearing dates, links to compiled testimony PDFs, status.
Output: bills.csv + downloaded bill texts.

Phase 2 — Testimony corpus:
Download all testimony PDFs for Phase 1 hearings. Extract text (OCR where
needed). Output: testimony/ tree + testimony_index.csv.

Phase 3 — Coding:
Code each testimony: testifier name, organization, org type (utility,
landholder/trust, ag lobby, enviro NGO, solar industry, county/state agency,
individual resident), position (support/oppose/comments/amend), specific ask
(cap tighter/looser/unchanged, SUP requirements, soil-class rules), and
whether boilerplate (near-duplicate text across testifiers — flag clusters).
Schema fields: bill, year, hearing_date, committee, testifier, org, org_type,
position, ask, boilerplate_cluster_id, pdf_path, page.

Phase 4 — Cross-reference:
- Hawaii Ethics Commission lobbyist registry: match orgs/firms.
- Campaign Spending Commission: donations to sponsors of restriction bills.
- LUC dockets (luc.hawaii.gov): intervenor and attorney signature blocks.
- PUC CDMS: intervention filings in contested solar dockets.
- HEI proxy statements + trust trustee rosters (interlock check).
Output: entities.csv (resolved), edges.csv (org–bill–position;
attorney–client; lobbyist–principal), for network analysis in R/Julia.

Phase 5 — Analysis & writing:
Timeline narrative (1976 ban → 2011 capped opening → 2014 partial
re-liberalization → adjustment wars → 2024–26 agrivoltaics fights);
testimony-position matrix by interest group; boilerplate/coordination
analysis; contrast of solar vs wind statutory treatment; report findings
whatever they show.
