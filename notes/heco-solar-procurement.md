# HECO's Utility-Scale Solar Procurement Machinery: How Projects Are Solicited, Selected, Approved, Delayed, and Killed

Compiled 2026-07-19. Companion data: `data/heco_procurement_timeline.csv`.
Primary sources cached in `data/raw/procurement/` (see `MANIFEST.md`). Extends
`notes/project-pipeline-mortality.md` (which establishes that HECO procurement —
not land-use permitting — is where Oahu solar dies). This note documents the
procurement *mechanism* itself.

**Evidentiary discipline.** Every claim is primary-sourced or flagged
**UNVERIFIED**. I report **revealed conduct** — who filed, who terminated, what
the record states. I do not report intent. The process-machinery quotes below are verified
against the cached HECO Final Stage 2 Oahu Variable RFP
(`stage2_oahu_variable_rfp.pdf`, extracted `.txt`) and the two cached PA
Consulting Act 201 interconnection studies.

**The governing dockets, in one place.**
- **PUC Docket 2017-0352** — "the RFP Docket." *All three* procurement rounds
  (Stage 1, 2, 3) run under this single umbrella. Individual PPAs get their own
  sub-dockets for approval (e.g., 2018-0430…0436, 2019-0050, 2020-0127/0132/
  0136/0137/0139, 2022-0007).
- **PUC Docket 03-0372** — the **Framework for Competitive Bidding** (D&O 23121,
  filed 2006-12-08). The constitution of the whole system.
- **PUC Docket 2021-0024** — interconnection/delay tracking; where the "2 of 9
  on track" status filings live.
- **PUC Docket 2015-0389** — CBRE (community solar), a *separate* program.

**Community-Based Renewable Energy** is a *separate* program under **Docket

## 2. Selection, self-build, and the Independent Observer

### How bids are scored (primary source: Stage 2 Oahu RFP, Ch. 4)

- **Two-team split:** a Price Evaluation Team and a Non-Price Evaluation Team
  score separately. **Total score = 60% price + 40% non-price** (§4.4, verified
  line 1930).
- **Price** collapses to a single **Levelized Energy Price ($/MWh)** (or
  $/MW for storage), built from fixed/variable O&M, lump-sum payment, energy
  price, and net energy — the metric that puts IPP and self-build bids on one
  ruler.
- **Eight non-price criteria** (§4.4.2): (1) Community Outreach & Cultural
  Resource Impacts; (2) State of Project Development & Schedule; (3) Performance
  Standards; (4) Environmental Compliance & Permitting; (5) Experience &
  Qualifications; (6) Financial Strength & Financing Plan; (7) Contract
  Exceptions; (8) Guaranteed Commercial Operations Date (GCOD). **The first
  three are weighted twice as heavily** (verified line 1986). Scored 1–5;
  category winner gets **400 points**, others prorated.
- **Fatal-flaws disqualification:** any proposal failing minimum standards
  (a score of 3) on **four or more** non-price criteria is disqualified
  (verified lines 1225, 2001–2042).
- **Deliverability / interconnection is handled separately** from the price/
  non-price score via a Chapter-5 **Interconnection Requirements Study (IRS)**;
  projects interconnect at 46 kV or 138 kV and may not create a single point of
  failure >135 MW (§1.2.7). **GCODs "given preference in scoring"** (verified
  line 445) — schedule certainty is explicitly rewarded.
- **Eligibility floor: "Projects must be greater than 5 MW"** (§1.2.7, verified
  line 348) — see §2c on the 5 MW pivot.
- Stage 3 expanded the non-price set to **eleven** metrics (Life of the Land /
  Ililani reporting — Tier 2; confirm against the Stage 3 RFP if the exact
  count is load-bearing).

### The self-build option and its conflict (primary source: §1.9)

HECO may bid **its own utility-owned projects** ("Self-Build Option," SBO) into
the RFP **it administers and scores**. The RFP names the conflict outright
(§1.9, verified lines 722–726): the Code of Conduct guards against "(1)
preferential treatment … of the Company formulating the SBO (the 'Self-Build
Team'); and (2) preferential access to proprietary information of the Self-Build
Team." Controls the record shows imposed:

- **Code of Conduct + Procedures Manual**, "required under the Framework" and
  "approved by the PUC in Docket No. 03-0372" (verified line 2685; cached
  `code_of_conduct_oahu_firm.pdf`); modified by Order 36112 (2019-01-24, docket
  2018-0065).
- **Self-Build Option Team Certification Form** (Appendix G) attesting no
  information advantage; **timestamp control** by the Energy Contract Manager
  "in consultation with the Independent Observer."
- **IO oversight** of Code-of-Conduct adherence; optional shared-savings
  mechanism that "will need to be approved by the PUC" and is **not scored**;
  a **volume cap** on SBO under HECO's "Parallel Plan."

**Revealed conduct on self-build:** self-build appears at **increasing scale**
across stages — Stage 2 = two storage self-builds (Waena, Keahole); Stage 3 =
the 253 MW **Waiau Repower**, the largest single Oahu award. PA Consulting's
Act 201 Phase 2 study flags the governance gap directly: self-build and IPP
interconnection costs are **"not comparable"** and recommends HECO "develop
comparable interconnection cost metrics for self-build … [and] track the total
interconnection cost of the self-build projects separately … so that … can be
compared with the IPP-built projects" (verified `pa_consulting_act201_phase2.txt`
lines 156–159). Per that study only **one qualifying self-build** (reported as
West Loch Solar) existed in the 2015–2022 interconnection window — UNVERIFIED
detail worth a clean read. The single offtaker also
competes in, and scores, the auction, and its own cost reporting is not on the
same footing as the IPPs it evaluates.

### The Independent Observer (IO)

**The IO is Bates White, LLC** — PUC-*appointed*, not HECO-appointed (Stage 2
RFP §1.4.3, verified line 570; §1.4.1: "The PUC has retained an Independent
Observer both to advise and monitor the process … All phases … subject to the
Independent Observer's oversight"). Enumerated duties (§1.4.2): monitor all
steps and communications, **monitor adherence to the Code of Conduct**, review
HECO's "evaluation methodology, models, criteria, and assumptions," monitor
contract negotiations, and **"Report to the PUC on monitoring results during
each stage."** Bates White partner **Vincent Musco** "directed the evaluation of
more than 75 proposals" across Oahu/Maui/Hawaii (~900 MW + 210 MW grid services)
(Bates White engagement page). Stage 1 IOs appointed by **Order 35224
(2018-01-12)**; UNVERIFIED that Bates White specifically was the *Stage 1* IO
(named in Stage 2; firm claims program-wide).

**IO findings (Tier 2 — the actual reports are on CDMS, not yet pulled).** Via

### 2c. The Framework and the 5 MW pivot (Docket 03-0372)

The **Framework for Competitive Bidding**, **D&O 23121 (2006-12-08, Docket
03-0372)**, is the enabling instrument the RFP repeatedly cites (Stage 2 RFP
§1.1.2, verified line 300; footnote line 831). The Framework lets the PUC
**waive** competitive bidding "upon a showing that the waiver will likely result
in a lower cost supply of electricity," and it exempts small additions from the
bidding requirement. The RFP's own **">5 MW" eligibility floor** (§1.2.7) is the
operational face of that de-minimis threshold: below ~5 MW, generation historically
routed to the Feed-in Tariff / non-RFP paths rather than the competitive auction.
The precise Framework applicability clause (secondary sources render it as "the
lesser of 5 MW or 1% of the utility's firm capacity") should be quoted directly
from D&O 23121 — Tier 2 on the exact numeric phrasing, **Tier 1 on the 5 MW
figure**, which is independently corroborated inside the paper's own record:
**HSCR1152 (2011)** states "the electric utility companies do not have to obtain
competitive bids for independent power producers that produce under five
megawatts," and derives the **20-acre** solar cap from it (≈4 acres/MW × 5 MW ≈
20 ac) — see `notes/sb631-2011.md`. **This is the hinge that ties the paper's
land-use cap to the procurement regime:** the §205-4.5 20-acre ceiling was sized
to the competitive-bidding waiver, i.e., to keep permitted-as-of-right solar
*below* the threshold that would pull it into HECO's RFP machinery. The cap and
the auction are the same 5 MW line drawn in two statutes.

## 3. The approval chain and its timelines

Full sequence for a third-party project:
**RFP issued → bids due → Final Award Group → PPA negotiation (under IO
oversight) → PPA execution → PUC PPA approval (D&O) → Interconnection
Requirements Study + Interconnection Agreement → Notice to Proceed →
construction → COD.**

Quantified durations from the cached PA Consulting Act 201 studies (the PUC's
own commissioned diagnostic; Phase 1 2022-12-28, Phase 2 2023-12-28):

- **IRS process alone** (award → filing of IRS Amendments): **~24 months
  average for Stage 1, ~21 months for Stage 2**; HECO *targets* **~12 months**
  for Stage 3 (`pa_consulting_act201_phase1.txt` lines 890, 1438–1441).
- **Time-to-date for projects still under development:** **~30 months (Stage 1),
  ~20 months (Stage 2)** as of the study — before COD (Phase 1 line 890).
- **RFP-issue to COD, observed:** Stage 1 ran **Feb 2018 → COD 2022–2025**,
  i.e., roughly **4 to 7.4 years** (Hoohana, the last, hit COD 2025-07-11 —
  ~7.4 years). Stage 2 ran **Aug 2019 → COD 2024–2026** for survivors, i.e.,
  **~5 to 7 years**. **Median observed award-to-COD for the built Oahu cohort
  ≈ 5–5.5 years for the Stage-1 built cohort** (award mid-2018; CODs 2022–2025); the built Stage-2 awards (Kupono, Kapolei Energy Storage) ran ≈4.1 years, pulling the all-built median to ≈4.4–4.6. Against original
  PPA targets ~3 years — a **typical slip of ~2–4 years**, and worse (4 years
  and counting) for Mountain View / Waiawa Ph2.

The PUC PPA-approval step is itself **low-mortality but not instant**: the PUC
"almost never denies a solar PPA" (`project-pipeline-mortality.md`), and Oahu
drew **zero** contested PPA dockets — but approval + IRS + interconnection
agreement stack up the months, and every month of that stack is exposure to the
cost shocks in §4.

## 4. Delays: how much, and why (per the filings)

**The magnitude, on the record.**
- **Oahu: 2 of 9** solar+storage projects "on track," 7 delayed — a **July 2022
  report to the PUC**, via Hawaii Public Radio 2022-08-22.
- **Statewide: 2 of 18** HECO agreements on track — a **2022-05-20 PUC filing**,
  via S&P Global (May 2022). Both trace to status filings in **Docket
  2021-0024** (opened Feb 2021 precisely because the PUC was concerned HECO's
  interconnection process was causing "unnecessary delays and increasing costs"
  — Utility Dive 2021-03-10; exact filing IDs UNVERIFIED, CDMS pull needed).

**The causes named in filings (revealed, sourced):**
1. **COVID supply chain + equipment inflation (2021–22):** solar-module prices
   **+57% (2021)**, shipping **~+500%** (Rystad, via Civil Beat 2022-05-15).
   HECO's **Q2-2022 Form 10-Q** (SEC EDGAR) attributes delays and **four
   null-and-void Stage-2 PPAs** to supply-chain disruption, CBP solar-product
   port detentions, and site conditions; the **FY2022 10-K** updates that to
   **five Stage-2 null-and-void + one mutual termination**.
2. **AD/CVD anti-circumvention probe:** Commerce opened it **2022-03-28** (Auxin
   petition); the Biden 24-month duty bridge landed **2022-06-06** (both dates in
   the HECO 10-Q). Froze module imports mid-build.
3. **PPA price rigidity vs. cost inflation:** on **2022-02-15** HECO filed
   amendments to two Stage-2 PPAs for delay + higher price; the **PUC declined
   both on 2022-03-02**; the developer (Longroad) then declared both PPAs **null
   and void 2022-05-05/06** and walked (Mahi/Pulehu). The approved-price PPA is
   effectively **not re-openable** without PUC assent, and when assent was
   withheld the projects died rather than repriced.
4. **Interconnection process itself** — the PA Consulting diagnosis (see below).
5. **Developer creditworthiness as a *delay* cause: NOT FOUND / UNVERIFIED.**
   The documented developer failure mode is *walking away when a price amendment
   is denied*, not the developer's own credit. (HECO's credit is the separate,
   Stage-3 story — §5.)

**The IDRP and what actually diagnosed the delays.** The PUC established the
**Interconnection Dispute Resolution Process, Order 39163 (2023-04-18)** "in
light of delays that Stage 1 and 2 projects encountered." Order 39163 is
*procedural* — it sets up a three-level dispute
process (Level 1 Independent-Engineer-facilitated negotiation ~30 business days;
Level 2 IE mediation; Level 3 escalation to the PUC by Order), and the IE
**cannot overrule** the utility (grid-safety liability stays with HECO). Order
39163 does **not** itself contain an evidentiary "why were they late" findings
section — **do not cite Order 39163 for a serialization finding.** The actual
diagnosis lives in the **PA Consulting "State of Hawaii Interconnection Process
Study,"** commissioned under **Act 201 (SLH 2022)**, whose §1(d)(9) expressly
ordered documentation of "the delays in the interconnection process for Stage 1
and Stage 2 … including the cause of each delay as well as the party responsible"
(verified `pa_consulting_act201_phase2.txt` lines 839–840). PA Consulting is
*also* the PUC's Stage-3 Independent Engineer — same firm authored the study and
built the IDRP.

PA Consulting's findings on delay cause (Phase 1 §1.2.6, verified):
- HECO attributes most delay to the **first interconnection step — developer
  model submissions with "multiple deficiencies"** that could not be used in the
  System Impact Study (SIS), forcing rework with model consultants/manufacturers.
- **Serialization is explicit and primary-sourced:** "since the system impact
  studies are performed as a **cluster** of projects, **if one project model is
  delayed in meeting its requirements, it delays the SIS for the rest of the
  projects in that cluster as well**" (line 925). One slow project taxes the
  whole cohort.
- **Post-SIS changes trigger re-study** — "any changes a developer may elect to
  incorporate … after completion of the SIS will require a re-study" — named the
  largest single delay driver.
- Process defects: **unclear/confusing interconnection requirements**,
  **communication delays/errors across multiple HECO teams**, single-step
  (rather than multi-step) data collection at bid, and **Rule 19 vs. Rule 14
  tariff ambiguity**. Most IPP projects were rated **"red"** (expected to miss
  guaranteed COD), **averaging ~6 months** of slip in the interconnection stage
  specifically (Phase 2). PA recommended **continuing the IDRP beyond Stage 3**
  and adding a **PIM for utility-caused delay** (the record previously penalized
  only developer-caused delay — an **asymmetric-penalty** finding).

## 6. Structural diagnosis (evidence-based)

What in HECO's procurement design lengthens timelines and causes attrition —
each stated plainly and sourced:

1. **Monopsony single-offtaker with a rigid approved price and a termination
   lever.** One buyer sets the PPA; once the PUC approves a price it is
   effectively **not re-openable** (HECO's 2022-02-15 amendments *denied*
   2022-03-02 → Longroad walked). When input costs spiked, projects had no
   pressure-relief valve except cancellation. The buyer also holds a unilateral
   termination right (2016 SunEdison). *Source: SEC 10-Q/10-K; Civil Beat 2022;
   Environment Hawaii.*
2. **The offtaker competes in — and scores — its own auction (self-build).**
   HECO bids utility-owned projects into an RFP it administers; the safeguards
   are a Code of Conduct + IO monitoring, but PA Consulting finds self-build and
   IPP **interconnection costs are "not comparable"** and cost reporting not on
   equal footing, and self-build's share of the award stack **grew** to the
   largest single Oahu Stage-3 award (Waiau Repower, 253 MW). *Source: Stage 2
   RFP §1.9; PA Consulting Phase 2 lines 156–159; status board.*
3. **Sequential, multi-year RFP stages** stretch procurement across a decade
   (Stage 1 2018 → Stage 3 2023; CODs to 2033), so any given project sits
   exposed to macro cost shocks for **~5–7 years RFP-to-COD**. Re-procurement
   (Mahi, Pulehu) restarts the clock rather than repricing in place. *Source:
   PA Consulting timelines; status board.*
4. **Interconnection-study serialization + restudy.** SIS is run in **clusters**;
   one deficient developer model **delays the whole cluster**, and any post-SIS
   design change **triggers a re-study** — named the largest delay driver. The
   IRS alone ran **~24 (Stage 1)/21 (Stage 2) months**. *Source: PA Consulting
   Phase 1 §1.2.6, lines 890, 925, 1438.*
5. **PPA price rigidity vs. cost inflation** (the mechanism inside #1): the very
   feature that protects ratepayers — a fixed, PUC-vetted price — becomes the
   kill switch when equipment inflation outruns it and the PUC declines to
   re-open. *Source: SEC filings; PUC 2022-03-02 denial.*
6. **Asymmetric accountability.** The process historically penalized
   **developer** delay but not **utility** delay in interconnection; PA
   Consulting recommended a PIM for utility-caused delay, and the PUC opened
   Docket 2021-0024 over HECO's interconnection process. *Source: PA Consulting
   Phase 2; Utility Dive 2021-03-10.*

**One-line diagnosis.** Oahu utility-scale solar dies inside a **monopsony
auction whose PUC-fixed price cannot flex to cost shocks, whose interconnection
studies serialize and restudy for two-plus years, and whose single offtaker also
competes in and scores the bids** — so projects sit exposed for 5–7 years and,
when inflation or the utility's own credit turns, developers walk (the utility
rarely has to terminate them itself).
