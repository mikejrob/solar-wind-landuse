## 1. The restriction inventory — four layers, two technologies

**Solar** (binding layer: HRS ch. 205, state):
- [V] 1976–2008 excluded from ag district (not an enumerated use); SUP only.
- [V] 2008 Act 31 (HB 2502): first entry, D/E soils only. An acreage cap
  (10 ac/1%) was proposed in committee, endorsed by DOA, and REMOVED.
- [V] 2011 Act 217 (SB 631): B/C opened, capped min(10% of parcel, 20 ac);
  class A banned. 10% = Senate SD1; 20-acre ceiling = House HD1, expressly
  sized to the 5 MW utility competitive-bid waiver.
- [V] 2014 Act 55 (SB 2658): SUP path above cap; 50%-below-FMR ag co-use
  lease + decommissioning conditions. Introduced as pure loosening (30%,
  no hard cap); tightened in Senate WTL. Act 52 (SB 2775): class-A road
  exception (sunset 2019). NO substantive solar-siting change since 2014.
- [V] County fiscal layer: Honolulu assessor reclassified operating solar
  farms ag→industrial in 2021 (~25x tax jump mid-PPA; 80% exemption patch).
  Ag dedication assesses at 1–5% of FMV — near-zero carrying cost for
  landowners holding ag land (subsidized land banking).

**Wind** (binding layer: county LUO):
- [V] State statute permissive since Act 24 (1980) — uncapped, unchanged 46
  years, enacted pre-PURPA-buildout, before any HI wind farm existed. (1980
  sponsors/testimony: paper journals only — archive visit pending. [U] HEI
  role plausible given its contemporaneous Kahuku wind ambitions.)
- [V] Honolulu Ord 25-2 (Bill 64, signed 1/3/2025): large wind must sit
  max(10× tip height, 1.25 mi) from residential/country/resort lots; DPP
  waivers banned; repowering ≤7%/575 ft within current PPA only. 18/20
  Kahuku turbines nonconforming; all ~155 MW of Oahu wind expected retired
  2031–2040. Proportionality: nearest turbine ~1,700 ft from a home;
  enacted setback 6,600 ft.
- [V] Setback geometry: 36% of Oahu AG/Country land remains viable vs 85%
  pre-2025 (before the waiver ban and repowering clamp bind further).
- [V] HECO publicly OPPOSED the 2020 state 1-mile setback bill (sole
  organizational opponent).

**Quantification of the solar cap** [V, GIS, notes/cap-quantification.md]:
- Oahu B/C ag soils: current law 3,601 ac ≈ 720 MW → "20% no hard cap"
  15,657 ac ≈ 3,131 MW (4.3×). Statewide 4.6×.
- THE HARD 20-ACRE CAP IS THE BINDING ELEMENT: 10%-no-ceiling alone
  triples eligibility; 20%-with-ceiling adds almost nothing.
- The ceiling binds on just 85 Oahu / 595 statewide parcels (all >200 ac);
  ~85–89% of liberalization gains accrue to large parcels (Parker Ranch,
  DHHL, KS, State lands identifiable).

## 2. Who built and maintains the solar cap (revealed conduct)

- [V] 2008 and 2011 and 2014, the tighten-side testimony is invariably:
  Dept of Agriculture, Office of Planning, county planning (DPP), Farm
  Bureau(s) — plus Sierra Club in 2011. The cap is an
  AG-PRESERVATION/PLANNING-ESTABLISHMENT construct.
- [V] The liberalize side, when present: DBEDT/energy office, solar
  industry (Sopogy, HSEA, First Wind), AND the utility (2008: HECO/MECO/
  HELCO supported), AND large landholders (2008: LURF and Castle & Cooke
  supported; 2011: Castle & Cooke supported the uncapped bill).
- [V] HECO/HEI, LURF, KS: zero testimony appearances on ch. 205 solar
  bills 2011–2025. The utility's only public land-use conduct is
  pro-siting (2008 support; 2020 opposition to wind setbacks; support for
  a solar SUP extension).
- [V] The one developer fingerprint on a restriction: First Wind Solar
  Group (KS's tenant at Waiawa/Kawailoa) REQUESTED the 50%-below-FMR
  co-use lease condition in 2014, crediting LUC and Sierra Club drafting
  help. Interpretations range from making-SUP-passable to
  raising-rivals'-costs; we report the conduct only.
- [V] Farm Bureau's stated economics (2011): solar outbids agriculture for
  land; "highest and best use" raises lease rents; farmers (largely
  TENANTS) lose. The cap functions as tenant protection / land-price
  control against a higher-value competing use.

## 3. Hypothesis scoreboard — end of day one

**H1 Scarcity rents to large landholders: RESTATED, NOT REFUTED.**
- Landholders did not create the caps (they testified for liberalization
  when they testified at all), and cap REMOVAL would be a large-parcel
  windfall (GIS). But they are the caps' principal incumbents-beneficiaries:
  eligibility under current law still concentrates on multi-parcel owners;
  the SUP path above the cap is navigable by sophisticated owners (LUC solar
  SUPs draw ZERO intervenors and clear in ~6 weeks) while the permitted-use
  route rations everyone else; ag dedication makes holding land nearly free.
  H1 survives as an INCIDENCE claim (who collects the rents the regime
  creates). It is not an AUTHORSHIP claim.

**H2 Rate-base/utility interest: NO DIRECT SUPPORT; INDIRECT STRUCTURE
CONFIRMED; MECHANISM UNPROVEN.**
- Direct conduct is exculpatory: supported 2008 liberalization; opposed wind
  setbacks; zero dollars to the cap-inserting chairs (campaign-finance
  null); counsel appears only defending its own PPAs.
- But the influence architecture exists and is dense [V]: HECO executives
  ran/officered LURF (president FY2005-06; VPs multiple eras; member during
  the SB 631 session); four simultaneous HEI/HECO–KS trustee interlocks;
  three sitting HEI directors simultaneously on A&B's board during SB 631;
  Parker Ranch/Grove Farm/Campbell ties today; a retired HECO SVP chairing
  the LUC in 2025.
- The utility never needed land-use law — its levers are
  procurement, interconnection, and PPA terms (PUC-supervised). Land
  restrictions were the ag coalition's project. Whether elite integration
  translated into quiet acquiescence is not observable in documents we have.

**H3 Astroturf: NULL, NOW ON FOUR INDEPENDENT TRACES.**
1. Legal representation: every community intervenor used the same small
   public-interest bar; no utility/landholder client overlap. Mirror
   finding: the LANDHOLDER bar (Castle & Cooke's LUC firm) works FOR solar
   developers.
2. Campaign finance: no interested-money pattern around restriction votes.
3. Boilerplate: the only text-copying found was Sierra Club's own
   legislative chair (intra-org, disclosed-role coordination).
4. Wind opposition: organic beyond reasonable doubt (arrests, residents,
   KCA; HECO on the other side).
A null this consistent is a publishable finding.

**H4 (new, emergent) — CHOKEPOINT SUPPLY / REPURPOSED ANTI-DEVELOPMENT
CAPACITY.** The connective tissue between economic forces and grassroots:
- Hawaii's multi-layer land regime (state district, county LUO, county tax,
  PUC) offers every organized constituency a veto point at its own margin.
- Grassroots capacity is built in OTHER fights and repurposed at near-zero
  cost: Sierra Club's ag-land absolutism was forged fighting Koa Ridge/
  Ho'opili housing (their food-security language was state-supplied
  vocabulary — CTAHR studies, IAL acts, OP/DOA strategy — wrapped around an
  anti-sprawl commitment strategy); North Shore wind opposition inherits the
  Keep-the-Country-Country/Turtle Bay lineage. Prediction: opposition
  intensity tracks pre-existing organization; project externalities do not
  drive it.
- The ag-preservation coalition (agencies + farm lobby + enviro) supplies
  restriction as a JOINT PRODUCT of fights about
  housing, sprawl, land prices, and tenancy — solar and wind are collateral.

## 4. What this does to the paper

1. The single-puppet-master narrative (utility astroturf) is dead on the
   evidence; say so plainly. Replace with: (a) an ag-coalition authorship
   story for solar's caps, (b) an incidence story for landholder rents,
   (c) an elite-integration story (LURF officership, interlocks) as the
   channel by which utility and landholder interests could coordinate
   without public conduct — reported as structure, with motive not
   asserted, and
   (d) chokepoint-supply as the unifying mechanism.
2. The Ord 25-2 wind story is the cleanest case of restriction overshoot:
   grievance at 1,700 ft, remedy at 6,600 ft + waiver ban + repowering
   clamp = technology foreclosure. Pair with the GIS geometry (85%→36%).
3. The reform target is now precise: THE 20-ACRE HARD CAP, not the
   percentage. One-line statutory change, 3–4× the permitted-use resource,
   with distributional consequences (large-parcel windfall) that explain
   the politics of why it hasn't happened — and suggest pairings (e.g.,
   cap-removal + ag-tenant protections or tax-recapture) that could split
   the blocking coalition.
4. Null results to report: astroturf (4 traces), HEI campaign
   money, landholder authorship of caps, LUC-as-bottleneck for solar
   (SUPs are fast and uncontested; the binding frictions are the statute
   itself, PUC/PPA contestation, county permits, and tax risk).

## 4c. Oahu integrated screen (ownership × soil class × transmission)
(added end-of-day; sources: notes/oahu-transmission-screen.md,
notes/oahu-ownership.md, data/oahu_owner_class_transmission.csv)

- [V] Ownership: 99% of Oahu ag-district acreage attributed via RPAD bulk
  table. Government holds 50.1% (State 25.8% incl. DLNR/ADC, federal 19.6%,
  county 2.7%, DHHL 2.0%). KS = 12.8% overall = 26.1% of private acres;
  private top-20 = 71.2% of private acreage; Gini 0.978.
- [V] Grid: 75% of B/C acreage within 3 km of a mapped 46kV+ line; 72% of
  S3(cap-reform)-eligible acres within 1 km. Transmission expansion is NOT
  the Oahu unlock; the >3km clusters are mountainous or Navy land. 46kV
  under-mapping means these are conservative (land is CLOSER than measured).
- [V] Near-grid D/E (uncapped, SUP-free): 20,359 ac within 1 km (~4,100 MW
  @5ac/MW) — decomposes mainly to federal/Navy (8,249 ac, Lualualei),
  KS (6,206), DHHL (1,661), Laukiha'a ag CPR (2,372). The zero-legal-
  friction resource is majority government + KS land.
- [V] Cap-bound parcels (>200 ac w/ B/C): USA, KS, Dole, State, Laukiha'a
  top the list; the hard cap removes ~5,800 eligible ac on these parcels;
  KS forgoes ~1,180 ac — on Oahu, KS is the largest private LOSER from the
  cap (scarcity-rent framing: restrictions bind on one's own land too).
- [P] Policy synthesis: cap reform's near-grid gains flow disproportionately
  to state/federal/KS land. A state-lands solar leasing program (ADC/DLNR)
  + cap removal is the land-side lever the state itself controls, and the
  most direct way to thicken PPA-bid competition without touching private
  estates; federal (Navy) land is the big wildcard.

## 4d. State-land solar (added; notes/state-land-solar.md)
- [V] Legal authority exists and is adequate: §171-95 (2002) non-auction
  65-yr renewable leases; §171-95.3 (2009) direct negotiation; Act 53 (2024)
  removed sell-to-utility requirement; ADC exempt from auctions (§163D-16).
- [V] Happened piecemeal, never as a program: DHHL the main lessor (Kalaeloa
  Solar Two 2013 = first utility-scale solar on state land; Anahola 12 MW),
  UH West Oahu 12.5 MW (2024), DLNR's FIRST Oahu §171-95.3 solar disposition
  only now (Eurus Olai 20 MW, BLNR Jan 2026), ADC solar on Kauai but not
  Oahu (Audit 21-01: 1,700+ idle Oahu acres, zero solar mentions).
- [V] PLDC: Act 55 (2011, SB 1555, DELA CRUZ/SOLOMON — same Senate figures
  as SB 631) → repealed Act 38 (2013) after Sierra Club/OHA/KAHEA/Local 5/
  county councils mobilized. Third instance of repurposed land-politics
  capacity foreclosing a supply channel. (PLDC never mentioned renewables;
  causal chill on later leasing flagged UNVERIFIED.)
- [V] Ceded-lands chill documented for SALES (Act 176 2009 moratorium), not
  leases; live frictions = OHA revenue accounting + DHHL beneficiary
  politics.
- Verdict: what binds is fragmentation + inertia (every agency CAN lease,
  none is tasked to), defended by trust politics and the PLDC scar. Timely
  hooks: HB 778 (2025) study; HECO REZ government-lands workshops July 2026.
