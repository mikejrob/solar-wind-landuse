# Neighbor Opposition and Interconnection Rights for Hawaii Utility-Scale Renewables

Compiled 2026-07-19. Primary sources cached in `data/raw/neighbor-interconnection/`
(see `MANIFEST.md`). Companion notes: `sup-census.md`, `legal-representation-map.md`,
`project-pipeline-mortality.md`, `oahu-ownership.md`.

**Scope.** Part A = the procedural mechanisms an adjacent/neighboring landowner can use to
oppose a utility-scale solar or wind project, each pinned to governing authority and a real
example. Part B = the gen-tie/interconnection land-rights chokepoint and the eminent-domain
question. Every claim is primary-sourced or flagged **UNVERIFIED**. This note documents
*mechanisms*; the case catalogue is a separate agent's product.

# PART A — How a neighbor opposes a utility-scale renewable project

## The structural key (read first)

For a solar/wind project on Oahu ag land above the §205-4.5 cap, the land-use permit is a
**state Special Permit** under **HRS §205-6**: the *county planning commission* holds the
hearing and decides, and the **Land Use Commission (LUC)** ratifies for parcels >15 acres
(§205-6(d), cached `hrs_205-6.htm`). Two facts about that structure determine where a
neighbor can fight:

1. **Intervention is NOT available at the LUC for special permits.** LUC rule
   **HAR §15-15-53(a)** expressly carves out "special permit proceedings before the
   commission" from the intervention right, and **HAR §15-15-95(d)** directs that the SUP
   petitioner "shall comply with all of the rules of practice and procedure of the county
   planning commission" (cached `luc_har.txt`). The LUC's own 45-day review (§205-6(e)) is a
   record-based ratification, not a trial. **This is why the LUC solar-SUP census shows
   0 intervenors in 8 dockets** (`sup-census.md`) — not because no one could object, but
   because the LUC is the wrong venue by rule. The contested case happens at the **county
   planning commission**.
2. **A grant is not expressly appealable; only a denial or LUC modification is** (§205-6(e)).
   So a neighbor who wants to stop an *approved* project cannot simply appeal the grant — the
   neighbor must first become a *party* (via a contested-case intervention) to earn HRS
   §91-14 appeal rights. **Intervention is therefore the master key.** This is exactly the
   Paeahu mechanism.

## Mechanism 1 — County planning-commission SUP/CUP: notice and testimony

- **Authority.** HRS §205-6(b): the county PC "shall notify the land use commission and such
  persons and agencies that may have an interest in the subject matter of the time and place
  of the hearing," and each county sets its own hearing rules (`hrs_205-6.htm`). County rules
  add published + mailed notice. Example (primary source, County of Hawaiʻi PC Rules rev.
  2017, `hawaii_county_pc_rules.txt` §4-5): twice-published newspaper notice in two county
  papers, **plus the applicant must "serve notice to surrounding property owners and lessees
  of record"** and include a form explaining the contested-case right. Honolulu, Maui, and
  Kauai PC rules have equivalents (Maui MPC Rules §12-201-20; Honolulu PC Rules Subchapter 5 /
  Rule 2-18 — Honolulu PDF not reachable this session, flagged).
- **Standing to testify.** Anyone may submit written/oral testimony at the public hearing —
  no property interest required. This is the weakest form of participation: testimony alone
  creates no appeal rights and no obligation on the commission to make findings addressing it.
- **Oahu instances.** All Oahu solar SUP hearings (SP15-405/406, SP21-411/412) drew only
  scattered *individual* testimony — e.g., ~10 near-identical opposition emails and Dr. Kioni
  Dudley at SP21-411; zero organizational opposition; zero requests to elevate to a contested
  case (`sup-census.md`). **Blocking power: nil. Delay: nil.**

## Mechanism 2 — Contested-case hearing (THE lever)

- **Authority.** HRS ch. 91 (HAPA): §91-9 (contested-case notice/hearing rights, `hrs_91-9.htm`)
  and §91-14 (judicial review, `hrs_91-14.htm`). For an SUP the contested case is convened
  under the **county PC's** rules (per §15-15-95(d) above), not the LUC's.
- **The two-part test for a right to a contested case** (Hawaii Supreme Court doctrine, from
  *Pub. Access Shoreline Hawaii (PASH) v. Hawaiʻi County Planning Comm'n*, 79 Haw. 425 (1995),
  and *Kilakila ʻO Haleakala v. BLNR*, SCWC-13-0003065 (Haw. 2016)): a hearing is "required
  by law" — and its denial appealable — when (1) the proceeding is one the agency is required
  to conduct as a contested case (by statute, rule, or **constitutional due process**), and
  (2) the claimant has **standing**, i.e., a legally cognizable interest that will be injured.
  An SMA/SUP land-use permit proceeding is a "contested case within the meaning of HRS ch. 91"
  (*Mahuiki v. Planning Comm'n*, 65 Haw. 506 (1982)).
- **The standing / proximity test.** Standing to intervene requires an interest "clearly
  distinguishable from that of the general public." This is met by (a) a **property interest**
  in or near the land, or (b) **injury-in-fact** — an actual or threatened injury traceable to
  the action and redressable. *Mahuiki* squarely holds that **adjacent/abutting landowners**
  have standing because environmental and aesthetic harms are "special and personal" to them;
  proximity converts a generalized grievance into distinguishable injury. Codified in the
  intervention rules: County of Hawaiʻi PC Rule §4-6(b) admits a petitioner who shows an
  interest "clearly distinguishable from that of the general public," "some property interest
  in the land," or "actual or threatened injury in fact" (`hawaii_county_pc_rules.txt`); the
  LUC's parallel rule §15-15-52(c)(2)/(f)(2) asks for the "property interest in the land" or
  direct-and-immediate effect, and if "an abutting property owner, the tax map key
  description" (`luc_har.txt`). Native-Hawaiian traditional/customary practitioners have
  standing independent of land ownership (PASH; PC Rule §4-6(b)(5)).
- **How to petition.** File a written motion/petition to intervene with a filing fee and by a
  short deadline before the hearing (County of Hawaiʻi: $200, ≥7 days before the first
  meeting, §4-6(a); LUC boundary-amendment analog: $50, within 15 days of published notice,
  §15-15-52(e),(h); Maui MPC §12-201-20: ≥10 days before hearing; PUC analog below). The
  commission rules on intervention *before* proceeding, and **denial of intervention is
  independently appealable to the circuit court under HRS §91-14** (County of Hawaiʻi
  §4-6(c); LUC §15-15-52(k)).
- **LUC intervention rule — citation correction.** The task referenced "HAR §15-15-71" for
  LUC intervention; **§15-15-71 is actually "Substitution of parties."** The LUC intervention
  rules are **§15-15-52** (boundary amendments) and **§15-15-53** (other proceedings) — and
  §15-15-53(a) *excludes special permits*, so for SUPs the operative intervention rule is the
  **county** PC rule, not the LUC's. (Verified against `luc_har.txt` table of contents and
  section text.)
- **The canonical example — Paeahu Solar (Maui).** The Maui Planning Commission **denied**
  Pono Power Coalition and the Maui Meadows Neighborhood Association intervention in the
  county SUP/SMA contested case. On appeal, **2nd Circuit Judge Kelsey Kawano ruled the
  denial improper, VACATED the county permits, and remanded with orders to grant
  intervention** (~late Jan 2022; Maui News/Maui Now, 2022-01-28). The holding: the
  neighbors met the standing test and were wrongly excluded; the permits could not stand while
  they were denied party status. That remand — mediation, hearing officer, contested-case
  hearing beginning Nov 2022 — injected the delay that (with COVID-era cost inflation) killed
  the project when HECO/Innergex terminated the PPA on 2023-11-28 (`project-pipeline-mortality.md`).
  **Blocking power: high (by delay, not by defeat-on-merits). Delay: 18–24 months.**
- **Oahu instances: ZERO.** No Oahu utility-scale solar project has drawn an intervention
  petition at any county PC (`sup-census.md`, `project-pipeline-mortality.md`). The
  opposition bar that runs these (Collins, Isaki, Lizzi, Hurley) appears **only on Maui**
  (`legal-representation-map.md`).

## Mechanism 3 — Chapter 343 (HEPA) environmental-review challenge

- **Authority.** HRS ch. 343. **HRS §343-5(a)(9)(E) lists a "power-generating facility" as an
  independent trigger for an environmental assessment** (`hrs_343-5.htm`) — so utility-scale
  solar/wind generally requires an EA (and an EIS if significant impacts are found), separate
  from any state-land or conservation-district trigger. A determination (a Finding of No
  Significant Impact, or EA/EIS acceptance) can be **challenged in court**; HRS §343-7 sets
  short statutes of limitation (a 60-day window to challenge an agency's failure to prepare an
  EA/EIS; 60 days from public notice of a FONSI/negative declaration; 30 days from EIS
  acceptance). Article XI, §9 of the Hawaii Constitution also affords a private right of action
  to enforce environmental laws (*Ala Loop*, cited in the §205-6 annotations, `hrs_205-6.htm`).
- **Standing.** Broad in the environmental realm — Hawaii courts "have not been inclined to
  foreclose challenges … through restrictive applications of standing" (*Mahuiki*; *PASH*).
  A neighbor asserting environmental/aesthetic injury generally qualifies.
- **Blocking power / delay.** Moderate. A HEPA suit rarely kills a project on the merits but
  is a **delay and cost vector**; the substance (wildfire, flooding, cultural, endangered
  species) overlaps with the contested-case arguments and is usually litigated together
  (Paeahu's neighbors raised all of these). **UNVERIFIED nuance:** whether a *private* solar
  farm on private ag land using no state funds/lands is caught by §343-5(a)(9)(E) alone, as
  opposed to needing a second trigger, deserves a clean read of the case law and OEQC
  guidance — the statutory text reads as an independent trigger, but confirm before asserting.
- **Oahu instances.** No Oahu solar HEPA challenge in the record. (The Oahu mass-mobilization
  and its litigation went to **wind** — Na Pua Makani/Kahuku — over HCP/incidental-take and
  zoning, not §343 solar review; `legal-representation-map.md`.)

## Mechanism 4 — Appeal to Circuit Court and appellate courts (HRS §91-14)

- **Authority.** HRS §91-14 (`hrs_91-14.htm`): "Any person aggrieved by a final decision and
  order in a contested case … is entitled to judicial review." Review is filed in circuit
  court within 30 days; §205-6(e) also makes a **denial/LUC-modification** of an SUP directly
  appealable to circuit court. Appellate review runs to the ICA/Supreme Court.
- **Standing to appeal.** Two prerequisites (*Mahuiki*): (1) injury from the action and
  (2) **participation as a party in the proceeding** — i.e., you must have intervened. This is
  why Mechanism 2 is the gate: **no party status ⇒ no §91-14 appeal.**
- **Delay it creates.** Large. Crucially, **§91-14(c) does NOT stay the agency decision
  automatically**; a stay requires a four-part showing (likelihood of success, irreparable
  harm, no public harm, public interest). But the *pendency* of the appeal — plus any remand —
  is itself the weapon: Paeahu's county-permit vacatur + remand consumed ~2 years. The Paeahu
  PUC-side appeal (below) ran Oct 2020 → March 2022.
- **Oahu instances.** None against Oahu solar. All Hawaii solar land-use appeals in the record
  are Maui (Paeahu).

## Mechanism 5 — PUC PPA-docket intervention (weaker for neighbors)

- **Authority.** HRS ch. 269; PUC Rules of Practice **HAR ch. 16-601**: **§16-601-55**
  (intervention → full party status) and **§16-601-57** (participation without intervention)
  (`puc_har_16-601.pdf`). To intervene, a movant must plead a financial/property/other
  interest, how it is affected, and how its interest "differs from those of the general
  public"; the window is short (≈20 days from published notice) and intervention "shall not be
  granted except on allegations reasonably pertinent to and [that] do not unreasonably broaden
  the issues."
- **Why it is weaker for neighbors.** The PUC docket reviews the *power-purchase agreement*
  (rates, prudence, public-trust balancing under HRS §§269-6(b)/269-145.5(b)) — not siting.
  Neighbors' physical-proximity harms (viewshed, flooding) are only indirectly cognizable.
  In **Paeahu**, the PUC granted Pono Power **participant** status (recognizing members'
  constitutional clean-and-healthful-environment interest) but **denied full intervenor
  status**; the Hawaii Supreme Court **AFFIRMED the PUC** in *In re Maui Electric Co.*,
  SCOT-21-0000041 (2022-03-02, Eddins J.; Wilson J. dissenting), holding the PUC discharged
  its public-trust duty and rejecting the neighbors' antitrust ("common counsel"/rule-of-
  reason) and precautionary arguments as speculative. **The neighbor arguments lost at the
  PUC and on appeal** — the PUC track is not where opposition bites.
- **Blocking power: low.** The PUC almost never denies a solar PPA; intervention there buys
  delay and a settlement lever (see Kahana's $1.375M community-benefits agreement,
  `project-pipeline-mortality.md`), not a veto. **Oahu instances: zero** community-intervened
  solar PPA dockets.

## Mechanism 6 — Weaker/private levers

- **Nuisance / private tort** (glare, drainage, viewshed, noise): common-law action in circuit
  court; no special land-use standing needed but requires proof of substantial unreasonable
  interference and typically yields damages/injunction only post-hoc. Viewshed alone is a weak
  nuisance theory in Hawaii. **No documented Hawaii solar nuisance suit.** (Wind noise is the
  live analog — the Kahuku grievance — but that fed *setback legislation*, not nuisance suits;
  `wind-setbacks.md`.)
- **County grading/building-permit objections**: a neighbor can raise drainage/erosion
  objections during grading-permit review (Honolulu ROH ch. 14; grading ordinance), but these
  are ministerial engineering permits with narrow discretion — low blocking power.
- **Property-value / "diminution" claims**: not an independent cause of action; folded into
  nuisance or offered as testimony. No standing to *block* on this basis.
- **Blocking power: low across the board; delay: low-moderate.** These are supplements to
  Mechanisms 2–4, not standalone stoppers.

## Why Oahu utility-scale solar has attracted none of these — hypothesis test

The "big single-owner parcels away from residences" hypothesis **holds and is the best
explanation**, on the SUP-census + ownership evidence:

- **The Oahu solar-SUP roster is entirely large legacy landholders on interior ag land:**
  Kamehameha Schools (Kawailoa SP15-406), Castle & Cooke/Renewables Land Holdings (Waipiʻo
  SP15-405), UH (AES West Oahu SP21-411), Monsanto/Hartung/Fat Law's in the Kunia corridor
  (Mahi Solar SP21-412) (`sup-census.md`). These are **thousands-of-acres parcels** — median
  host ~2,950 ac — where the array sits far from any residential subdivision, so there is no
  abutting owner with the *proximity injury* that confers §91-14 standing under *Mahuiki*.
  Contrast Paeahu: 150 ac on Ulupalakua Ranch **directly mauka of the Maui Meadows
  subdivision** — the neighbors literally abutted, which is what gave them standing.
- **Ownership concentration removes the objector class.** `oahu-ownership.md`: government +
  KS + a handful of corporate-ag owners hold the near-grid B/C ag land; ~50% of Oahu's ag
  district is government-owned. Where one large owner controls the site (and often the
  surroundings), the set of adjacent private owners who could intervene is thin. The largest
  private landholders are **lessors monetizing siting rights, not opponents**
  (`legal-representation-map.md` nulls).
- **The projects that could face neighbors instead route *around* the whole state process:**
  Kupono on federal Navy land (no ch. 205), Hoʻohana via urban-district boundary amendment,
  several Central Oahu farms on D/E soils that need no SUP at all (`sup-census.md` "dogs that
  didn't bark").

Net: on Oahu the neighbor-opposition mechanisms exist and are potent (Paeahu shows what they
do) but the **precondition — a proximate, non-lessor adjacent owner with distinguishable
injury — is essentially absent** because Oahu utility-scale solar sits on large interior
parcels held/surrounded by institutional owners. Oahu solar dies upstream in HECO's
procurement economics (`project-pipeline-mortality.md`), not at a neighbor's hand.

# PART B — Interconnection rights through neighboring property

## B1. Securing the gen-tie route (the anticommons chokepoint)

A project's generator-tie ("gen-tie") line must run from the plant to the utility's **Point
of Interconnection (POI)**. When the POI is not on the project parcel, the line crosses
**other owners' land**, and the developer must assemble **easements/rights-of-way** for it.
HECO's tariff makes the land-rights burden the developer's: **Rule 15** provides that where a
customer's premises are served across separate property, "the customer must provide an
easement or right-of-way" of a form that would satisfy a utility (`heco_rule15.txt`), and
**Rule 14 §F** puts on the interconnecting customer the "sole risk and expense" of all wires
and equipment required to deliver energy to the Company's lines "regardless of the location"
of the Company's equipment (`heco_rule14.txt`). Because each intervening parcel owner can
refuse or hold out for a premium, a multi-parcel route is a classic **anticommons**: any
single holdout can block or reprice the whole line. This is a real, if largely undocumented,
siting constraint distinct from the array footprint itself.

## B2. Can an IPP condemn a gen-tie easement? — **Generally NO** (the crux)

**Authority: HRS §101-4** (`hrs_101-4.htm`). Eminent domain is granted to "**every person,
operating a public utility**, and engaged in … the production, conveyance, transmission,
delivery, or furnishing of electricity … within the State." The operative gate is **operating
a public utility.**

**A merchant renewable IPP selling wholesale to HECO under a PPA is not a "public utility"**
within HRS §269-1 (`hrs_269-1.htm`): the definition reaches persons furnishing power
"**directly or indirectly for public use**," and a generator that sells to a single
buyer (the utility) — not to the public — has not historically been treated as a regulated,
certificated public utility in Hawaii. The statute even enumerates renewable-generator
**exclusions** — §269-1(2)(M) and (N) (renewable systems on a customer's/owner's own property
selling to a utility or on-site tenants). Because an IPP is not "operating a public utility,"
**it cannot invoke §101-4, and therefore an IPP cannot itself condemn a gen-tie easement.**
It must acquire the route by voluntary easement (or route to avoid holdouts).

**By contrast, HECO — the certificated public utility — DOES hold §101-4 eminent-domain
power** for its electric transmission/delivery facilities. The practically important, and
**UNVERIFIED**, questions are: (i) whether HECO would exercise condemnation to acquire a
gen-tie/interconnection corridor that principally benefits one IPP, and (ii) **at whose cost**
— i.e., whether the "public use" requirement is satisfied and whether HECO would (or the PUC
would let it) socialize versus assign the condemnation cost to the IPP. No Hawaii case
squarely decides whether an IPP falls inside or outside §101-4, and **no documented instance
of HECO condemning land for an IPP gen-tie was found** this session. Treat the condemnation
answer as: **IPP — no (high confidence, on the §101-4/§269-1 text); HECO-on-IPP's-behalf —
legally possible but undocumented and cost-uncertain (UNVERIFIED).**

## B3. HECO interconnection framework and cost allocation

- **Rule 14H** ("Interconnection of Distributed Generating Facilities Operating in Parallel,"
  Rule 14 §H; current version PUC Docket **2019-0323**, Orders 38070 (2021)/40670 (2024);
  built on **IEEE 1547-2018**) governs interconnection to HECO's **distribution** system
  (`heco_rule14.txt`). It defines **Interconnection Facilities** as "the electrical wires,
  switches and related equipment … required in addition to the facilities … to provide
  electric distribution service … to allow interconnection," which "may be located on either
  side of the Point of Interconnection" and "may be integral to a Generating Facility or
  provided separately." (Large transmission-level IPPs interconnect via a project-specific
  facilities/interconnection agreement tied to the PPA and an **Interconnection Requirements
  Study (IRS)**, rather than the retail Rule 14H path, but the cost-allocation principle is
  the same.)
- **Cost allocation — the interconnecting generator pays.** Rule 14H (Sheet at
  `heco_rule14.txt` line ~3492): "**The Customer shall pay to the Company the total estimated
  interconnection cost** … comprised of (i) the estimated cost of the **Company
  Interconnection Facilities**, (ii) … engineering costs … [of] developing the Company
  Interconnection Facilities and … reviewing … the Facility." Any distribution/transmission
  **system upgrades** the IRS identifies are likewise assigned to the interconnecting
  generator. **Ownership:** facilities the Company builds/installs remain Company property
  (Rule 14 §D, `heco_rule14.txt`), even though the interconnecting customer funds them; the
  gen-tie up to the POI is the developer's to build, own (or convey), and — critically — to
  **secure land rights for** (Rule 14 §F; Rule 15). So: **developer funds essentially the
  whole interconnection (its gen-tie + HECO's interconnection facilities + upgrades); HECO
  owns the utility-side facilities; the developer bears the easement-assembly risk.**
- **Rule 2 / Rule 15 line-extension mechanics** (Rule 15 cached; Rule 2 not retrieved this
  session, 404): line-extension/cost-contribution rules generally require the applicant to
  advance costs above a standard allowance and to furnish easements — reinforcing that
  land-rights and interconnection cost fall on the developer, not ratepayers, at the margin.

## B4. Does holdout risk vary by island / ownership concentration? — **Yes (inferred)**

The gen-tie holdout hazard is a direct function of how many *distinct* owners a route crosses:

- **Oahu near-grid ag land is highly concentrated** (`oahu-ownership.md`): government + KS +
  Dole + a few corporate-ag owners dominate the Central Oahu / Kunia / North Shore corridors
  (Gini 0.978; top-10 owners = ~57% of B/C acreage; ~50% government). When **one owner
  controls both the site and the likely route** (e.g., a KS or Castle & Cooke block, or a
  project routing within Mahi Pono / Dole lands), there is **no intervening third party to
  hold out** — the developer negotiates a single lease + easement package. This is the same
  concentration that removes the neighbor-*opposition* class in Part A, and it cuts the same
  way for interconnection.
- **Fragmented ownership raises the hazard.** Routes crossing many small parcels (e.g.,
  ag-CPR condominiums like Laukihaʻa, or smallholder plats near Waialua/Haleiwa) multiply the
  veto points. On the neighbor islands, and in any Oahu corridor that leaves an institutional
  block, the anticommons risk is materially higher.
- **A real Hawaii gen-tie routing instance (partial):** at **Kahuku wind**, the interconnecting
  transmission line's routing was itself a permitting subject — the Honolulu DPP granted a
  **zoning variance to *shorten* the new interconnecting line**, and the PUC approved an
  **overhead transmission line in Oct 2017** (Civil Beat 2019/2024 reporting). This shows the
  gen-tie as a real, separately-permitted, contested element (shortening it drew the turbines
  nearer the community) — but it is a *permitting/variance* record, **not** a documented
  easement-holdout or condemnation.

  **UPDATE (2026, from the project census, `notes/hawaii-project-census.md`): a documented
  instance now exists.** **Puʻu Hao Solar** (Maui, AES, HECO Stage 3) was withdrawn
  **2024-05-31** because the developer, per the record, *"could not secure the land to
  interconnect"* — the gen-tie easement anticommons biting in the open, exactly as the theory
  above predicts. It is a project *death* attributed to interconnection-land access, and the
  first Hawaii example located of this mechanism actually *biting*. (Island note: Maui, not
  Oʻahu — consistent with Oʻahu's near-grid ag land being concentrated enough that site+route
  often sit with one owner; the fragmented-ownership holdout risk showed up where the theory
  said it would.) The earlier "no documented Hawaii instance" statement is superseded by this
  case; the condemnation question (whether an IPP could have forced the easement — no, per
  §101-4) is unchanged and is precisely why AES had to walk.

## B5. Documented vs. inferred (Part B honesty ledger)

- **Documented (primary source):** IPPs are not "public utilities" and §101-4 grants
  condemnation only to persons "operating a public utility" (`hrs_101-4.htm`, `hrs_269-1.htm`);
  the interconnecting generator pays HECO's interconnection cost and provides easements/ROW
  (Rule 14 §D/§F, Rule 15, Rule 14H cost sheet); Rule 14H rides on IEEE 1547 via PUC Docket
  2019-0323.
- **Inferred (well-grounded but not case-proven):** an IPP therefore cannot condemn a gen-tie;
  ownership concentration lowers Oahu holdout risk; Kahuku's line-routing illustrates gen-tie
  contestability.
- **UNVERIFIED / gaps:** whether HECO would/ could condemn a corridor chiefly benefiting one
  IPP and at whose cost (no Hawaii case; no documented instance); whether §343-5(a)(9)(E)
  alone triggers HEPA for a wholly-private solar farm; per-project interconnection costs and
  any gen-tie easement disputes (confidential / not in searched record); the exact Honolulu
  and Maui PC intervention-rule text (PDFs not reachable — Hawaiʻi County rule used as the
  representative primary source).

# The Oahu bottom line (both questions)

- **Neighbor opposition.** The only mechanism with real teeth is the **county
  planning-commission contested-case intervention** (HRS §91-9/§91-14 + county PC rules), and
  it works by **injecting delay** that becomes fatal when it meets a cost shock — the Paeahu
  model. Its precondition is an **abutting/proximate private owner with injury "distinguishable
  from the general public"** (*Mahuiki*). On Oahu that precondition is essentially unmet:
  utility-scale solar sits on **large interior parcels held or surrounded by institutional
  owners** (government, KS, Dole, C&C, Mahi Pono) who are lessors, not neighbors — so **zero**
  Oahu solar projects have ever drawn an intervention, appeal, HEPA suit, or PUC contested
  case. Organized solar opposition is a **Maui** phenomenon; Oahu solar dies of HECO
  procurement economics upstream.
- **Interconnection.** The gen-tie is the developer's to build, fund, and **acquire land
  rights for** (HECO Rule 14/15; interconnecting generator pays HECO's interconnection-facility
  cost per Rule 14H). **An IPP cannot condemn an easement** — HRS §101-4 grants eminent domain
  only to a person "operating a public utility," which a wholesale PPA seller is not (HRS
  §269-1); only HECO holds that power, and whether it would wield it for an IPP gen-tie (and at
  whose cost) is **undocumented**. Holdout risk is **low on Oahu precisely because near-grid
  ag land is so concentrated** that one owner often controls both site and route — the same
  concentration that erases the neighbor-objector class also collapses the interconnection
  anticommons.
