# SB 631 (2011) → Act 217: The origin of the 10%/20-acre solar cap

**Dossier status: verified against primary documents** (bill texts, committee
reports, testimony PDFs, archived measure status page). All source files cached
in `data/raw/capitol/` and `testimony/sb631_2011/`; originals retrieved via
Wayback Machine snapshots of capitol.hawaii.gov (live site blocks non-browser
clients as of July 2026).

## Bottom line

The 10%-of-parcel cap was inserted by the **Senate** committees (SD1, not HD1
as previously assumed — CLAUDE.md corrected). The "or twenty acres, whichever
is the lesser" hard ceiling was added by the **House EEP/WLO** committees
(HD1). The documentary record shows the cap's constituency was the
**agricultural-preservation / planning establishment** — Office of Planning,
Department of Agriculture, Honolulu DPP, Hawaii Farm Bureau — **not** the
utility or large landholders. **HECO/HEI never testified on SB 631 at any of
its three hearings. Neither did LURF or Kamehameha Schools.** The large
landowner on the record, Castle & Cooke, testified in support of the
*uncapped* introduced bill.

The one utility-linked thread: the House committees' **stated rationale for
the 20-acre figure** was to size permitted solar to ~5 MW — the threshold
below which "the electric utility companies do not have to obtain competitive
bids for independent power producers" (HSCR1152). I.e., the cap was calibrated
to the utility's competitive-bidding waiver so farmers could sell to HECO
bilaterally. Framed as farmer-friendly; the effect is that utility-scale solar
remained impossible as a permitted use.

## Measure facts

- Introduced 1/21/2011 by **Solomon (Malama), Ige (David), Kahele, Kidani,
  Kim, Slom**. Referred ENE/AGL/WLH (Senate); EEP/WLO → AGR (House).
- Became **Act 217**, signed 7/11/2011.
- Source: archived measure status page (Wayback snapshot of
  `capitol.hawaii.gov/session2011/lists/measure_indiv.aspx?billtype=SB&billnumber=631`,
  cached as `data/raw/capitol/sb631_measure_indiv.html`).

## Version-by-version cap language (verbatim, §205-2(d)(6))

| Version | Date reported | Language |
|---|---|---|
| Introduced | 1/21/2011 | No cap. Renewable energy production permitted "whether or not agricultural activity is the primary activity"; solar on all soil classes. |
| SD1 (SSCR489) | 3/3/2011 | "(A) …only to land with…rating class B, C, D or E; (B) Solar energy facilities placed within land with soil classified as overall productivity rating class B or C **shall not occupy more than ten per cent of the acreage of the parcel**; (C) …shall not be placed in areas designated as Important Agricultural Lands…" |
| HD1 (HSCR1152) | 3/24/2011 | (B) amended: "…ten per cent of the acreage of the parcel, **or twenty acres of land, whichever is the lesser**"; (C) IAL ban softened with appurtenance exception. |
| HD2 (HSCR1426) | 4/6/2011 | Substance unchanged; effective date pushed to 7/1/2020 to force conference ("to encourage further discussion"). |
| CD1 (CCR9) | 4/28/2011 | 10%/20-acre lesser-of retained; IAL references deleted (conflict with class-D IAL where solar already unrestricted); new §205-4.5(a)(19) mirror provision banning class A; effective on approval. |

Note within the same amended list: item "(8) Wind machines and wind farms;" —
**no conditions of any kind**. The solar/wind asymmetry is visible inside a
single subsection of the enacted text.

## Votes

- Senate 3rd reading (SD1): 22–2 (Noes: Hee, Slom).
- House 3rd reading (HD2): unanimous (2 excused).
- Senate final (CD1): 23–2 (Noes: Ihara, Slom).
- Committee votes all unanimous ayes. **The cap was consensus, not contested.**

## Committee-stated rationales (the only stated intent in the record)

- **SSCR489 (Senate, 10% cap):** "…the construction of renewable energy
  facilities on agricultural land should be limited to a certain extent in
  order to balance the need for energy security with the need for food
  security."
- **HSCR1152 (House, 20-acre ceiling):** "Under existing law, the electric
  utility companies do not have to obtain competitive bids for independent
  power producers that produce under five megawatts of power. As it takes
  approximately four acres of photovoltaic panels to produce one megawatt…
  approximately 20 acres of land would produce five megawatts… Allowing the
  use of agricultural land for renewable solar energy production provides
  farmers and ranchers with the opportunity to produce and sell electricity to
  offset their costs." — The 20-acre number is derived from the utility
  competitive-bidding waiver threshold; the permitted use was conceived as
  farmer-scale distributed generation, not utility-scale IPP.

## Corrections to prior working assumptions (CLAUDE.md)

1. **10% cap origin: Senate SD1** (ENE/AGL/WLH; Gabbard/Nishihara/Dela Cruz),
   not House HD1. HD1 added only the 20-acre lesser-of ceiling (+IAL
   appurtenance exception).
2. **Solar was not fully banned on all good ag land 1976–2011:** Act 31
   (HB 2502, SLH 2008) had already made solar a permitted use on **D and E**
   (marginal) lands (per DOA testimony; §205-2(d)(6) pre-2011 text restricted
   solar to D/E). The 1976→2011 exclusion story holds for A/B/C. (Act number
   confirmed = Act 31; see notes/pre2011-solar-bills.md. The earlier "Act 231"
   was an OCR/transcription artifact.)
3. **No utility or trust fingerprints on the SB631 record.** The
   scarcity-restriction was built by ag/planning agencies + Farm Bureau, with
   enviro (Sierra Club) opposition to liberalization. Rate-base hypothesis has
   no direct SB631 evidence; its only hook is the 5 MW bid-waiver calibration.
   Astroturf hypothesis: nothing industry-side; the only text-copying found is
   individuals echoing the Sierra Club.

## Political-economy readings consistent with this record

- **Farm Bureau's economics are land-market economics:** their stated fear is
  solar's superior willingness-to-pay for land — higher lease rents
  ("highest and best use") pricing farmers out. The cap functions as rent
  control on ag land against a higher-value competing use; incumbent ag
  *tenants* (Farm Bureau constituency) benefit; landowners with development
  options lose option value — consistent with Castle & Cooke supporting the
  uncapped bill.
- The scarcity-rent story for large landholders must therefore run through
  **later conduct** (defending caps once in place, SUP-process advantages),
  not through creating the 2011 cap. Watch LURF/landholder testimony in
  2014/2015/2016/2018/2021/2022 amendment fights.
